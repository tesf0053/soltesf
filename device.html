Rising action

The main action of the play comprises the struggle of Willy Lowman, a salesman by profession. The rising action occurs when Willy is fired from the job and the falling action occurs when Willy blames himself for the troubles his family is going through.

Allegory

Death of a Salesman shows the use of allegory by presenting the main idea of how the person creates trouble for himself and the people who belong to him.

Anaphora

The play shows the use of anaphora at different places for example: 1, Maybe I oughta get stuck into something. Maybe that’s my trouble. I’m like a boy. I’m not married, I’m not in business, I just—I’m like a boy. Are you content, Hap? You’re a success, aren’t you? Are you content? (Act-I) 2, Willy: Where is he? I’ll whip him, I’ll whip him! Linda: And he’d better give back that football, Willy, it’s not nice. Willy: Biff! Where is he? Why is he taking everything? (Act-I) These examples show the repetitious use of the phrases “you’re no good”, “only think”, “I’ll whip him”, and “I’m like a boy.”

Allusion

The play shows the use of different allusions as given in the examples below, 1, Biff: But you look at your friend….Happy: Yeah, but when he walks into the store the waves part in front of him… I want to walk into the store the way he walks in. (Act-I) 2, Willi: Like a young god, Hercules- something like that. And the sun, the sun all around him. Remember how he waved to me? Right up from the field, with the representatives of three colleges stand by? And the buyers I brought and the cheers when he came out, Loman, Loman, Loman. God Almighty, he’ll be great yet. (Act-I)

Conflict

There are two types of conflicts in the play, Death of a Salesman. The first one is the external conflict that is going on between Willy Loman and the world around him as well as his own family. Another is the internal conflict of Willy, his fight with the hard odds of life, and about the troubles of his life and how they are going to be resolved.

Charley

Charley Willy’s neighbor, is concerned about Willy, and he repeatedly offers him a job. Charlie is a true friend to Willy, even though Willy is jealous of him. Charley appears in Willy's memories, as well as in the actions of the present.

Climax

The climax of the play, Death of a Salesman, occurs when Willy confronts his distressed son, Biff, for the last time.

Irony

The play shows situational irony in the following examples, 1, That’s just what I mean. Bernard can get the best marks in school, understand, but when he gets out in the business world, understand, you are going to be five times ahead of him. That’s why I thank Almighty God you’re both built like Adonises. Because the man who makes an appearance in the business world, the man who creates personal interest, is the man who gets ahead. Be liked and you will never want. (Act-I) 2, CHARLEY (an arm on Bernard’s shoulder): How do you like this kid? Gonna argue a case in front of the Supreme Court. The irony is clear in the first example through the mention of Adonises and in the second through the mention of the Supreme Court.

Metaphor

Death of a Salesman shows good use of various metaphors besides the extended metaphors of good versus evil such as, 1, I am a dime a dozen, and so are you. (Act-II) 2, The world is an oyster, but you don’t crack it open on a mattress. (Act-I) These examples show that characters and the world have been compared to different things to make them feel noticable.

Mood

The Play, Death of a Salesman, shows a melancholic, though it becomes tragic, and ironic Sometimes, it also becomes gloomy when Willy is faced in the troubles of life.

Protagonist

Willy Loman is the protagonist of the play. The text starts with his discontent with his life and ends with his death.
